# What Is It Like to Be a Bat?

> **Source:** *The Philosophical Review*, Vol. 83, No. 4 (Oct. 1974), pp. 435–450  
> **Author:** Thomas Nagel  
> **Published:** 1974  
> **Category:** Philosophy of Mind | Foundational | Phenomenal Consciousness

---

## TL;DR

The subjective character of experience — what it is *like* to be a particular creature — cannot be captured by any objective, third-person description. Even if we knew everything about bat neuroscience and echolocation, we still wouldn't know what it's *like* to be a bat. This irreducibility of the subjective suggests that physicalism, as currently understood, is incomplete.

---

## The Central Argument

### The Bat Case
Bats navigate by echolocation — emitting ultrasonic pulses and perceiving the world through their reflections. This is a radically different sensory system from anything humans have.

Nagel asks: **what is it like to be a bat?**

No matter how much we learn about bat neuroscience, sonar physics, or behavioral ecology, we seem unable to answer this from the outside. We could imagine what *we* would experience if we had echolocation, but that's just imagining a human's experience of echolocation — not a bat's experience. Bats have their own subjective point of view, fundamentally inaccessible to us.

### The Subjective Character of Experience
Nagel introduces the phrase **"what it is like"** as a marker of phenomenal consciousness:

> "An organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something it is like to be that organism — some subjective character of experience."

The bat has experience. There is something it is like to be a bat, navigating by sonar in the dark. This "what it is like" is the target of inquiry — and it seems to resist objective characterization.

### The Objectivity Problem
Science proceeds by making descriptions more **objective** — removing the perspective of the individual observer, finding facts that hold from any viewpoint. Temperature, wavelength, neural firing rates — all objective.

But phenomenal consciousness is irreducibly **subjective**: it essentially has a point of view. The redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the character of echolocation — these can only be fully grasped from the inside. Any attempt to describe them purely objectively seems to leave out the very thing we're trying to describe.

---

## The Other Minds Problem, Sharpened

Nagel's argument sharpens the traditional **other minds problem**:

- With *similar* beings (other humans), we assume their experience resembles ours closely enough to communicate meaningfully
- With *very different* beings (bats, octopuses, LLMs), the gap in subjective experience may be so radical that our concepts don't apply

We don't know what it's like to be a bat; we may also not know what it's like to be an AI — or even whether there is anything it is like at all.

---

## Implications for Physicalism

Nagel is not arguing for dualism. He's arguing that **current physicalist theories are incomplete** because they lack the conceptual resources to explain subjective experience. A complete physics of the brain would still not explain why those physical processes feel like anything.

This is a precursor to Chalmers' Hard Problem — Nagel diagnosed the gap; Chalmers named it and sharpened it.

---

## Relevance to AI Consciousness

Nagel's paper raises the most fundamental challenge for AI consciousness research:

**Even if LLMs pass every behavioral test, implement every functional indicator, and satisfy every theory-derived criterion — we still face the bat question:**

> Is there something it is like to be this model?

And crucially: **we may have no way to know from the outside.** The subjective character of a model's experience (if any) is precisely what cannot be captured by behavioral or functional descriptions.

This is why the AE Studio deception-suppression finding is particularly interesting — it's an attempt to find a third path: neither pure behavioral inference nor direct phenomenal access, but mechanistic investigation of whether self-reports track genuine internal states rather than performance.

---

## Nagel's Later Work

Nagel developed these themes in:
- *Mortal Questions* (1979) — essays on subjectivity, death, and ethics
- *The View From Nowhere* (1986) — extended treatment of objectivity vs. subjectivity
- *Mind and Cosmos* (2012) — controversial argument that neo-Darwinian materialism is fundamentally incomplete; consciousness requires a different naturalistic framework

*Mind and Cosmos* was poorly received by many scientists and philosophers, but the underlying concern — that consciousness doesn't fit neatly into the standard scientific picture — remains live.
