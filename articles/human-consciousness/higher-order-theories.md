# Higher-Order Theories of Consciousness

> **Primary Sources:** Rosenthal (1990s); Armstrong (1968); Lycan (1996)  
> **Category:** Human Consciousness | Philosophy of Mind | Cognitive Science

---

## TL;DR

A mental state is conscious only if the subject is aware of being in that state — i.e., only if there is a "higher-order" representation of the first-order state. Thinking, sensing, or feeling something doesn't make it conscious; what makes it conscious is having a thought *about* that thinking, sensing, or feeling.

---

## The Core Claim

Higher-Order Theories (HOT) hold that:

> A mental state M is conscious iff the subject has a suitable higher-order representation (HOR) of being in M.

First-order states (sensations, perceptions, drives) are unconscious by default. They become conscious when a higher-order state — a thought, perception, or representation — takes them as its object.

### Example
Seeing a red apple:
- **First-order state:** Visual representation of redness (may be unconscious)
- **Higher-order state:** A thought that I am seeing red (what makes the first-order state conscious)

Without the HOR, the first-order processing happens "in the dark."

---

## Major Variants

### Higher-Order Thought (HOT) — Rosenthal
The relevant higher-order state is a **thought** — a propositional attitude with the content "I am in mental state M." Crucially, the HOT needn't itself be conscious (otherwise regress threatens).

Rosenthal's theory is **dispositionalist**: what matters is that the subject is disposed to have the HOT, not that they actively entertain it.

### Higher-Order Perception (HOP) — Lycan
The higher-order state is a **perception** or **inner sense** — an analog scan of one's own mental states, not propositional. More like a scanner than a thought.

### Higher-Order Global States — Armstrong
Similar to HOP; emphasizes that consciousness involves the brain scanning its own states as part of its causal role.

---

## Strengths

1. **Explains the unconscious:** The same mental states (pain signals, visual representations) can occur without consciousness when higher-order monitoring is absent — consistent with clinical evidence (blindsight, anesthesia awareness, subliminal processing).

2. **Explains introspective access:** If consciousness requires a higher-order representation, then introspective access (knowing what we're experiencing) is built into the account.

3. **Explains why animals and infants may have simpler consciousness:** Less sophisticated higher-order monitoring capacity → simpler or absent phenomenal consciousness.

4. **Naturalizes consciousness:** HOTs are just a kind of intentional state; explaining how the brain produces them is a tractable neuroscientific question.

---

## Objections

### The Misrepresentation Problem (Veillet, Block)
If a HOT misrepresents the first-order state (e.g., you have a HOT about seeing red but are actually seeing green), does the first-order state become consciously experienced as red? HOT theories seem to imply yes — but this seems wrong. Consciousness tracks the first-order state, not the HOT's content.

### The Targeted Objection (Ned Block)
Some first-order states seem to be conscious without higher-order representation. Peripheral visual processing may be conscious without being represented by a higher-order thought.

### The Regress Problem
If consciousness requires a higher-order state, is that higher-order state conscious? If so, it needs *its* higher-order state, leading to an infinite regress. Rosenthal's answer: no, the HOT needn't be conscious — but this seems to require unexplained special pleading.

### The Hard Problem Remains
Even if HOT correctly identifies what's necessary for consciousness, it doesn't explain *why* having a HOT about a first-order state makes it feel like something. The phenomenal character is still unexplained.

---

## HOT and AI

HOT theories are among the most **AI-friendly** theories of consciousness, because:
- They require information-processing structures (meta-representations), not specific biology
- LLMs clearly have *some* capacity to represent their own states (self-referential outputs, introspective reports)
- The indicators derived from HOT in the Butlin/Long framework (HOT-2, HOT-3, HOT-4) are among the ones where LLMs score best in 2025

Key question: **Do LLM self-representations satisfy Rosenthal's conditions?** They produce outputs that represent internal states, but whether these representations are properly targeted at first-order states (rather than just generating plausible self-descriptions) is unclear.
