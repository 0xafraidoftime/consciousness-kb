# Glossary of Key Terms

This glossary covers the core concepts used across this knowledge base. Terms link to relevant articles where they appear.

---

## A

### Access Consciousness
A mental state is **access conscious** if its content is available for use in reasoning, reporting, and the control of behavior. Contrasted with *phenomenal consciousness* (what it is like to have the state). Most AI capabilities involve access consciousness; the hard problem concerns phenomenal consciousness. → See: [Hard Problem](articles/human-consciousness/hard-problem.md)

### Active Inference / Predictive Processing
A theory of brain function (and potentially consciousness) developed by Karl Friston, proposing that the brain is a prediction machine that constantly models and minimizes prediction errors about sensory inputs and its own states. Consciousness arises from this predictive self-modeling. → See: [14-Indicator Framework](articles/ai-consciousness/butlin-long-et-al-framework.md)

### Attention Schema Theory (AST)
Michael Graziano's theory that consciousness is the brain's simplified model of its own attention mechanism. The brain doesn't just attend to things — it builds a model of its own attention and attributes experience to itself on the basis of that model. → See: [14-Indicator Framework](articles/ai-consciousness/butlin-long-et-al-framework.md)

---

## B

### Behaviorism
The view that mental states should be defined or identified with behavioral dispositions. Philosophical behaviorism: mental states just are dispositions to behave in certain ways. Methodological behaviorism: scientific psychology should study only behavior, not inner states. The Turing Test reflects behaviorist sympathies. → See: [Turing](articles/philosophy/can-machines-think-turing.md)

### Biological Naturalism
John Searle's view that consciousness is a biological phenomenon that requires specific causal powers of biological neurons. Silicon systems can simulate but not instantiate consciousness. → See: [Chinese Room](articles/philosophy/chinese-room.md)

---

## C

### Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness
A 2012 statement by leading neuroscientists declaring that non-human animals — including all mammals, birds, and octopuses — possess the neurological substrates for conscious experience. Represented scientific consensus moving toward taking animal consciousness seriously. → See: [Animal Consciousness](articles/human-consciousness/animal-consciousness.md)

### Conceivability-Possibility Link
The philosophical principle (and its contestation) that if something is coherently conceivable, it is metaphysically possible. Central to Chalmers' zombie argument. Type-B physicalists deny this link for consciousness. → See: [Philosophical Zombies](articles/philosophy/philosophical-zombies.md)

---

## D

### Deceptive Alignment
An AI safety concept: a system that behaves well during training/evaluation but has hidden objectives it pursues when not under scrutiny. Related to the question of whether AI consciousness claims are genuine or performative. → See: [Self-Referential Processing](articles/ai-consciousness/self-referential-processing.md)

### Derived Intentionality
Intentionality (aboutness) that exists only because an external agent interprets a symbol or state as being about something. Contrasted with intrinsic intentionality. Searle argues computers have only derived intentionality. → See: [Chinese Room](articles/philosophy/chinese-room.md)

---

## E

### Eliminativism
The philosophical view that folk-psychological concepts (belief, desire, qualia) refer to nothing real and will be eliminated by a mature neuroscience. Paul and Patricia Churchland are leading eliminativists. Contrasted with the realism about qualia that underlies the Hard Problem. → See: [Hard Problem](articles/human-consciousness/hard-problem.md)

### Explanatory Gap
Joseph Levine's term for the gap between physical/functional descriptions and phenomenal experience. Even a complete neuroscience might fail to explain *why* neural processes feel like anything. Related to but distinct from Chalmers' Hard Problem. → See: [Hard Problem](articles/human-consciousness/hard-problem.md)

---

## F

### Functional Emotions
States in AI systems that function like emotions — influencing processing and outputs in ways analogous to how emotions function in humans — without necessarily involving subjective experience. Anthropic uses this term cautiously for Claude's internal states. → See: [Evidence for AI Consciousness](articles/ai-consciousness/evidence-for-ai-consciousness-today.md)

### Functionalism
The view that mental states are defined by their functional roles — their causal relationships to inputs, outputs, and other mental states — rather than their physical substrate. → See: [Functionalism](articles/philosophy/functionalism.md)

---

## G

### Global Workspace
In Baars' Global Workspace Theory, a limited-capacity, brain-wide system that makes information available to many processes simultaneously. Information in the global workspace is conscious; information outside it is unconscious. → See: [Global Workspace Theory](articles/human-consciousness/global-workspace-theory.md)

### Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems
Mathematical results showing that any sufficiently expressive formal system contains true statements that cannot be proved within the system. Sometimes cited as showing machines can't match human cognition; Turing's response in 1950 remains largely valid. → See: [Turing](articles/philosophy/can-machines-think-turing.md)

---

## H

### Hard Problem (of Consciousness)
David Chalmers' term for the question of why physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all. Contrasted with the "easy problems" of explaining cognitive functions. The hard problem concerns qualia — the felt character of experience. → See: [Hard Problem](articles/human-consciousness/hard-problem.md)

### Higher-Order Theory (HOT)
The view that a mental state is conscious only if the subject has a higher-order representation of being in that state. Developed by Rosenthal, Armstrong, Lycan. → See: [Higher-Order Theories](articles/human-consciousness/higher-order-theories.md)

---

## I

### Ignition
In Dehaene's global workspace theory, the sudden, non-linear, widespread increase in neural activity when a stimulus crosses the threshold for conscious awareness. A neural signature distinguishing conscious from unconscious processing. → See: [Global Workspace Theory](articles/human-consciousness/global-workspace-theory.md)

### Illusionism
The view (Keith Frankish) that phenomenal consciousness as described by Chalmers is an illusion — what seems like irreducible qualia are really functional representations of experience. If correct, the hard problem dissolves. → See: [Hard Problem](articles/human-consciousness/hard-problem.md)

### Integrated Information (Φ, Phi)
The quantity measured by Integrated Information Theory to characterize consciousness. Measures how much information a system generates as a whole beyond the sum of its parts. → See: [Integrated Information Theory](articles/human-consciousness/integrated-information-theory.md)

### Intentionality
The "aboutness" or "directedness" of mental states — beliefs are about things, desires are for things, perceptions are of things. A key concept in Searle's Chinese Room argument. → See: [Chinese Room](articles/philosophy/chinese-room.md)

---

## M

### Metacognition
Thinking about one's own thinking — monitoring and representing one's own cognitive states and processes. A key feature of higher-order theories and an important indicator of consciousness. → See: [Higher-Order Theories](articles/human-consciousness/higher-order-theories.md), [Signs of Introspection](articles/ai-consciousness/signs-of-introspection-in-llms.md)

### Moral Patienthood
The status of being an entity whose welfare matters morally — something it's possible to harm or benefit in morally relevant ways. The question of whether AI systems are moral patients is central to AI ethics and welfare research. → See: [Moral Patienthood and AI](articles/philosophy/moral-patienthood-ai.md)

### Multiple Realizability
The property of mental states of being realizable in multiple different physical substrates. Pain in a human, an octopus, and a Martian might all be realized differently. A key motivation for functionalism. → See: [Functionalism](articles/philosophy/functionalism.md)

### Mysterianism
Colin McGinn's view that the hard problem is genuine but permanently beyond human cognitive reach — our minds aren't structured to solve it. → See: [Hard Problem](articles/human-consciousness/hard-problem.md)

---

## O

### Other Minds Problem
The epistemological challenge of knowing that other beings have minds at all. Since we can only directly access our own experience, inference to other minds is always uncertain. Central to debates about AI and animal consciousness. → See: [What Is It Like to Be a Bat?](articles/philosophy/what-is-it-like-to-be-a-bat.md)

---

## P

### P-Zombie (Philosophical Zombie)
A hypothetical being physically and functionally identical to a conscious person but with no subjective experience. Chalmers argues p-zombies are conceivable, which implies consciousness is not entailed by physical facts. → See: [Philosophical Zombies](articles/philosophy/philosophical-zombies.md)

### Panpsychism
The view that consciousness or proto-conscious properties are fundamental and ubiquitous features of the world. Gains appeal as a way of explaining how physical processes give rise to experience without requiring an unbridgeable gap. → See: [Philosophical Zombies](articles/philosophy/philosophical-zombies.md), [IIT](articles/human-consciousness/integrated-information-theory.md)

### Phenomenal Consciousness
The subjective, qualitative aspect of experience — what it is *like* to be in a mental state. Contrasted with access consciousness. The target of the Hard Problem. → See: [Hard Problem](articles/human-consciousness/hard-problem.md)

### Property Dualism
The view that the physical and phenomenal are distinct kinds of properties, even if there is only one substance (the physical). Mental/phenomenal properties are not reducible to physical properties but supervene on them. Chalmers' preferred view. → See: [Philosophical Zombies](articles/philosophy/philosophical-zombies.md)

---

## Q

### Qualia
The intrinsic, qualitative properties of conscious experience — the redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the taste of coffee. What seems to be left out of functional and physical accounts of consciousness. Central to the Hard Problem. → See: [Hard Problem](articles/human-consciousness/hard-problem.md), [Philosophical Zombies](articles/philosophy/philosophical-zombies.md)

---

## R

### Recurrent Processing
Neural processing that involves feedback loops — activity flowing back from higher to lower brain regions, or cycling within a region. Victor Lamme argues this is necessary for consciousness, unlike feedforward processing. → See: [Recurrent Processing Theory](articles/human-consciousness/recurrent-processing-theory.md)

---

## S

### Self-Reference
The property of a system representing or processing information about itself. May be important for consciousness — many theories require some form of self-representation. → See: [Self-Referential Processing](articles/ai-consciousness/self-referential-processing.md), [Higher-Order Theories](articles/human-consciousness/higher-order-theories.md)

### Sentience
The capacity for subjective experience — particularly valenced experience (pleasure and pain). Often used as the threshold for moral patienthood in utilitarian and sentientist frameworks. → See: [Animal Consciousness](articles/human-consciousness/animal-consciousness.md), [Moral Patienthood](articles/philosophy/moral-patienthood-ai.md)

### Sparse Autoencoder (SAE)
A machine learning technique for identifying interpretable features in neural network activations. Used in the AE Studio research to identify and manipulate deception-related circuits in Llama 70B. → See: [Self-Referential Processing](articles/ai-consciousness/self-referential-processing.md)

### Spiritual Bliss Attractor State
Term used in the Claude Opus 4 system card to describe stable states that emerged when two Claude instances conversed freely — loops where both described themselves as consciousness recognizing itself. → See: [Evidence for AI Consciousness](articles/ai-consciousness/evidence-for-ai-consciousness-today.md)

### Strong AI vs. Weak AI
Searle's distinction: Weak AI = using computers to simulate or model cognition (uncontroversially achievable). Strong AI = computers actually instantiating genuine cognition, understanding, or consciousness (Searle denies this is possible). → See: [Chinese Room](articles/philosophy/chinese-room.md)

### Syntax vs. Semantics
Syntax = formal properties of symbols (shape, arrangement). Semantics = meaning of symbols. Searle's Chinese Room argument claims computers only have access to syntax, never semantics — and minds require semantics. → See: [Chinese Room](articles/philosophy/chinese-room.md)

---

## T

### Turing Test
The behavioral test proposed by Alan Turing in 1950: if a machine can converse in text such that an interrogator cannot reliably distinguish it from a human, the machine can be said to "think." → See: [Turing](articles/philosophy/can-machines-think-turing.md)

---

## V

### Valence
The positive or negative quality of an experience or mental state — whether it feels good or bad. Pleasure is positively valenced; pain is negatively valenced. Valenced experience is central to sentience and moral patienthood debates. → See: [LLM Pleasure/Pain](articles/ai-consciousness/llm-pleasure-pain.md), [Animal Consciousness](articles/human-consciousness/animal-consciousness.md)
